Understanding Email Phishing

Email phishing is the most common form of cyber attack, where criminals send fraudulent messages designed to trick people into revealing sensitive information or installing malware. These emails often appear to come from reputable companies or individuals you know.

Example of a phishing email attempting to steal login credentials

Common Characteristics of Phishing Emails:

  • Urgent or threatening language that creates a sense of panic
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Spoofed sender addresses that mimic legitimate companies
  • Requests for sensitive information like passwords, Social Security numbers, or credit card details
  • Suspicious attachments or links to fake websites
  • Poor spelling and grammar that legitimate organizations typically avoid

Did You Know?

According to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center, phishing was the most common type of cyber crime in 2022, with over 300,000 victims reporting losses exceeding $52 million.

How to Protect Yourself:

  1. Never provide personal information in response to an unsolicited request
  2. Verify the sender's email address carefully, not just the display name
  3. Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking
  4. When in doubt, contact the company directly using official contact information
  5. Use multi-factor authentication to protect your accounts

Correct! The main objective is to trick you into revealing information they can exploit.
Incorrect. Phishing is about theft, not legitimate business or security.
Recognize Fake Websites Locked

Identifying Fake Websites

Phishing emails often contain links to fake websites designed to look identical to legitimate ones. These sites are created to steal your credentials or install malware on your device when you interact with them.

Fake websites often look nearly identical to legitimate ones

How to Spot Fake Websites:

  • Check the URL carefully: Look for misspellings, unusual characters, or wrong domain extensions
  • Look for HTTPS: Legitimate sites use HTTPS (not HTTP) to encrypt data
  • Examine the padlock icon: Click on it to view security certificate details
  • Poor design quality: Many fake sites have low-quality images, layouts, or functionality
  • Suspicious content: Unexpected pop-ups, download prompts, or requests for unnecessary information

Typosquatting Alert

Cybercriminals often register domains with common misspellings of popular websites (like "gooogle.com" instead of "google.com") to trick users. This practice is called "typosquatting" or "URL hijacking."

Website Security Check:

  1. Always check the URL in the address bar before entering any information
  2. Look for the padlock icon indicating a secure connection
  3. Verify the website's security certificate if you're unsure
  4. Use a website reputation service if available in your browser
  5. Bookmark important sites instead of clicking links in emails
Key indicators of a safe website: HTTPS and padlock icon

Correct! The domain uses '1' instead of 'l' and lacks `https`.
Incorrect. Look closely at the spelling and the `http` vs `https`.

Correct! HTTPS means the connection is secure and encrypted, but you still need to verify the domain itself is correct.

Detect Suspicious Attachments Locked

Dangerous Email Attachments

Email attachments are a common method for delivering malware, including viruses, ransomware, and spyware. Cybercriminals often use social engineering to trick users into opening malicious attachments.

Malicious emails often disguise dangerous attachments as legitimate documents

Risky File Types to Avoid:

  • Executable files: .exe, .msi, .bat, .cmd, .scr
  • Script files: .js, .vbs, .ps1, .jar
  • Compressed files: .zip, .rar (may contain malicious executables)
  • Office documents with macros: .docm, .xlsm, .pptm
  • PDF files: Can sometimes contain malicious scripts or links

Ransomware Threat

Approximately 94% of malware is delivered via email, often through malicious attachments. Ransomware attacks frequently begin with an employee opening an infected attachment that encrypts the organization's data.

Safe Attachment Practices:

  1. Never open attachments from unknown senders
  2. Even with known senders, verify unexpected attachments before opening
  3. Use antivirus software to scan all downloads
  4. Keep software updated to protect against known vulnerabilities
  5. Enable file extension viewing in Windows to better identify file types
Common dangerous file types used in cyber attacks

Incorrect. While any file can be a risk, `.exe` files are programs and thus the most directly dangerous.

Check Email Grammar & Tone Locked

Language Clues in Phishing Emails

Many phishing attempts originate from non-native English speakers or use automated translation tools, resulting in grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and unusual tone. These language clues can help identify fraudulent messages.

Phishing email showing poor grammar and spelling mistakes

Language Red Flags:

  • Spelling and grammar mistakes: Legitimate companies typically proofread communications
  • Unnatural phrasing: Awkward sentence structure or unusual word choices
  • Overly formal or informal language: Inconsistent with the sender's typical style
  • Urgent or threatening tone: Creates pressure to bypass your critical thinking
  • Generic greetings: "Dear valued customer" instead of using your name

Psychological Manipulation

Phishers often use urgency, fear, or excitement to trigger emotional responses that override logical thinking. Messages about account suspension, unexpected prizes, or urgent requests from authority figures are designed to make you act without verifying.

"Hi team, please find the attached weekly report."
"Your monthly statement is now available to view online."
Correct! The poor grammar, spelling errors, and urgent tone are all huge red flags.
Incorrect. Professional communications are typically well-written.

To make their email stand out in a crowded inbox.
Correct! The sense of urgency is a psychological trick to bypass your rational judgment.
Incorrect. It's a tactic designed to make you act on emotion rather than logic.
Verify Sender Information Locked

Email Spoofing and Sender Verification

Cybercriminals often spoof email addresses to make messages appear to come from legitimate sources. Learning to verify sender information is crucial for identifying phishing attempts.

Examining email headers can reveal the true sender information

How to Verify Sender Authenticity:

  • Check the email address, not just the display name: Display names are easily faked
  • Look for slight misspellings in the domain: "amaz0n.com" instead of "amazon.com"
  • Examine the reply-to address: It may differ from the sender address
  • View email headers: Headers show the actual routing information
  • Be wary of free email services: Legitimate businesses rarely use Gmail or Yahoo for official communications

Domain Spoofing Techniques

Attackers use various techniques to create deceptive domains:
- Homograph attacks: Using similar-looking characters (e.g., "rnicrosoft.com" vs "microsoft.com")
- Subdomain deception: "microsoft.security-alert.com" appears legitimate at first glance
- Top-level domain variations: "amazon.co" instead of "amazon.com"

Which sender email address is the fake one?
support@microsoft-security.com
support@microsoft.com
noreply@email.microsoft.com
Correct! The official domain is `microsoft.com`. Scammers create similar-looking domains to trick you.
Incorrect. The key is the main domain. `microsoft-security.com` is not owned by Microsoft.
An email's display name is "Your Boss", but the address is "user123@gmail.com". What should you conclude?
Your boss is probably sending from their personal account.
It's safe because the display name looks correct.
This is highly suspicious and likely a phishing attempt.
Correct! The display name is easily faked. The email address is the real clue, and it's unlikely your boss would use a generic personal email for official business.
Incorrect. Always trust the email address, not the display name.
The Link Hover Test Locked

Inspecting Links Before Clicking

The text of a link can say anything, but the destination URL can be completely different. Always inspect links before clicking to avoid being directed to malicious websites.

Hover over links to see the actual destination URL

How to Inspect Links Safely:

  • Hover before clicking: On desktop, hover your mouse over the link to see the URL
  • Check for HTTPS: Ensure the URL begins with "https://" for secure connections
  • Examine the domain name: Look for misspellings or unusual characters
  • Be cautious of URL shorteners: Services like bit.ly can hide malicious destinations
  • Use link scanners: Browser extensions can automatically check links for safety

URL Deconstruction

Understanding URL structure helps identify fakes:
- Protocol: https:// (secure) vs http:// (insecure)
- Domain: paypal.com (legitimate) vs paypa1.com (fake)
- Subdomain: login.paypal.com (legitimate) vs paypal.login-security.com (fake)
- Path: /account/login (typical) vs /secure-login (suspicious)

Mobile Considerations:

On mobile devices, it's harder to hover over links. Instead:

  1. Long-press on the link to see the URL before opening
  2. Use official apps instead of clicking links in emails
  3. When in doubt, manually type the website address

Social Media Scams Locked

Social Media Phishing Threats

Social media platforms have become fertile ground for phishing attacks. Scammers use fake profiles, malicious links, and deceptive messages to trick users into revealing sensitive information.

Social media platforms are increasingly targeted by phishers

Common Social Media Scams:

  • Fake giveaway offers: "You've won a prize! Click here to claim"
  • Profile cloning: Scammers create copies of your friends' profiles
  • Malicious ads: Ads promoting fake products or services
  • Fake customer support: Impersonating company support accounts
  • Quiz scams: "Answer these questions to find out which character you are"

Profile Cloning

In profile cloning attacks, scammers copy profile pictures and information from legitimate accounts to create convincing fakes. They then send friend requests to the original account's contacts, often followed by messages requesting money or personal information.

Protecting Yourself on Social Media:

  1. Adjust privacy settings to limit who can see your information
  2. Be cautious of unsolicited messages, even from friends
  3. Verify unusual requests through another communication channel
  4. Don't overshare personal information that could be used in targeted attacks
  5. Use different passwords for social media accounts

Spear Phishing Locked

Targeted Spear Phishing Attacks

Spear phishing is a highly targeted form of phishing where attackers research specific individuals or organizations to create personalized, convincing messages that are more likely to succeed.

Spear phishing emails are personalized and often reference real colleagues or projects

Characteristics of Spear Phishing:

  • Personalized content: Uses your name, position, or other specific details
  • Apparent legitimacy: Appears to come from someone you know or trust
  • Research-based: References real projects, events, or colleagues
  • Higher quality: Better writing and design than generic phishing
  • Specific goals: Often aims to steal sensitive data or financial information

Whaling Attacks

"Whaling" is a type of spear phishing that specifically targets high-level executives. These attacks are particularly sophisticated and may involve extensive research to create convincing messages that appear to come from other executives or trusted partners.

Defending Against Spear Phishing:

  1. Be skeptical of unexpected requests, even from known contacts
  2. Verify unusual requests through a separate communication channel
  3. Limit the amount of personal information you share online
  4. Implement multi-factor authentication for sensitive accounts
  5. Provide regular security awareness training for employees

A generic newsletter you subscribed to.
A mass email announcing a 20% off sale.
Correct! This is highly personalized and asks for a high-risk action.
Incorrect. Spear phishing is defined by its personalization.

Correct! Because it uses details specific to you, it bypasses the "this seems generic" red flag, making it much more effective.

Phone & SMS Phishing (Vishing/Smishing) Locked

Voice and SMS Phishing

Phishing isn't limited to email. "Vishing" (voice phishing) uses phone calls, while "Smishing" (SMS phishing) uses text messages to trick victims into revealing sensitive information.

Phone-based phishing attempts are becoming increasingly common

Common Vishing and Smishing Tactics:

  • Impersonation: Posing as banks, government agencies, or tech support
  • Urgent requests: Claiming your account is compromised and immediate action is needed
  • Fake verification: Asking you to verify account details or passwords
  • Prize scams: Notifying you of a prize you've won with a link to claim it
  • Two-factor authentication bypass: Tricking you into providing verification codes

Caller ID Spoofing

Vishers often spoof caller ID information to make calls appear to come from legitimate organizations. They can make it look like the call is coming from your bank's official number, making the scam more convincing.

Protecting Against Phone-Based Phishing:

  1. Be skeptical of unsolicited calls or texts, even if they appear to come from legitimate numbers
  2. Never provide sensitive information over the phone unless you initiated the call
  3. Hang up and call the organization back using an official number from their website
  4. Don't respond to suspicious texts; delete them immediately
  5. Register your number on the National Do Not Call Registry to reduce unsolicited calls

Correct! Unsolicited texts with strange links are a very common form of smishing.

Reporting & Prevention Locked

Reporting Phishing Attempts

Reporting phishing attempts is crucial for protecting yourself and others. Your reports help security companies and service providers identify new threats and improve their filters.

Reporting phishing helps protect the wider community

How to Report Phishing:

  • Email providers: Use the "Report Phishing" or "Report Spam" button
  • Forward emails: Send phishing emails to reportphishing@apwg.org
  • Government agencies: Report to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov
  • Financial institutions: Forward bank-related phishing to the institution's fraud department
  • Social media platforms: Use reporting features on social media sites

Why Reporting Matters

When you report phishing attempts, you're contributing to a collective defense system. Security companies analyze reported phishing messages to identify new tactics, update filters, and sometimes even track down and disrupt phishing operations.

Proactive Prevention Measures:

  1. Use comprehensive security software with anti-phishing protection
  2. Keep all software updated to patch security vulnerabilities
  3. Enable multi-factor authentication on all important accounts
  4. Regularly back up important data to mitigate ransomware threats
  5. Educate yourself and others about the latest phishing techniques

The Golden Rule of Cybersecurity Locked

The Human Firewall

Ultimately, the most effective defense against phishing is you. Technology can provide important safeguards, but human vigilance and skepticism are the final line of defense.

Staying informed is your best defense against phishing

The Golden Rules of Cybersecurity:

  • Think before you click: Always verify before interacting with links or attachments
  • Verify requests: Confirm unusual requests through a separate channel
  • Stay informed: Keep up with the latest phishing techniques and scams
  • Use technology: Enable security features like multi-factor authentication
  • Trust your instincts: If something feels wrong, it probably is

Continuous Learning

Phishing tactics constantly evolve, so ongoing education is essential. Regular security awareness training can reduce successful phishing attacks by up to 70% according to recent studies.

Building a Security-Conscious Mindset:

  1. Make security part of your daily routine, not an afterthought
  2. Share knowledge with friends, family, and colleagues
  3. Participate in security awareness programs when available
  4. Stay curious about new technologies and their security implications
  5. Remember that everyone makes mistakes - what matters is learning from them
